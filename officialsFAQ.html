Welcome!

Glad to hear you're interested in joining us. There are several steps involved, but each is manageable on its own; just take things one step at a time. Answers to most questions you'll encounter can be found on the Gulf Swimming web page, but feel free to reach out to Alex Fuller (splash01@gmail.com) for help. Here are the steps:

  1. Create a USA Swimming account. Use this link to be associated with our team.

    • If you already are a member, let the Gulf Registrar, Loren Fichbach (loren.fischbach@gulfswimming.org), know your ID and that you'd like to be associated with FCST.

    • When you complete your training FCST will reimburse the cost of registration.

  2. Complete the Background Check.

    • USA Swimming, Gulf Swimming, and FCST take SafeSport seriously. The background check is one way we protect our kids.

    • Log into your USA Swimming account. Under the Education/Course Catalog menu, find the Background Check class. To easily find it, type "background" in the Search Courses by Name field. Complete the form for the background check.

    • When you complete your training FCST will reimburse the cost of the background check.

  3. Complete the SafeSport training class.

    • Log into your USA Swimming account. Under the Education/Course Catalog menu, find the Athlete Protection Training class by clicking on the Safe Sport icon and looking for "Athlete Protection Training."

    • Be a little bit careful here because there are quite a few courses. Some are labelled as "Refresher" courses, and you do not want one of those. Other courses include SafeSport for Volunteers and Parents Guide to Misconduct in Sport. Some of these you may even have taken previously. To become an official, you must take the "Athlete Protection Training" course.

Once you complete that much, we can allow you on deck to begin training. However, there are a couple of additional steps you'll need to complete before you can receive training credit for sessions:

  1. Complete Concussion Protocol Training.

    • This also begins in USA Swimming's Education/Course Catalog but goes to an external web site. You may take either the CDC version or the National Federation of State High School Associations course. You may have to create an account with one of these organizations.

    • When done, you'll receive a certificate and/or completion notice. Send that to Loren Fichbach (loren.fischbach@gulfswimming.org).

  2. Complete the Foundations of Officiating course and the Stroke & Turn courses.

    • Each of these will be an interactive course with a quiz at the end. The quizzes are open book and are designed to teach some of what you may see on deck, as well as the basic philosophy of officiating.

    • When completed, the courses will give you a completion certificate or notification. Send them to Natalie Melenric, our Gulf Officials Coordinator, at officials@gulfswimming.org.

At this point, you'll be considered an Apprentice Stroke and Turn official. The next steps are to obtain the uniform and to sign up for a meet. The meet referee will assign you one or more certified officials to "shadow." You'll be on deck and standing beside the official. They will point out what they are looking for during various events and you will be able to hear the disqualification phraseology. Take it slow, learn everything you can from each official. You're on deck and have the best seat in the house!

Do you look good in ?

For most meets, we wear a white polo or golf-style shirt with navy blue shorts, pants, or a skirt. Shoes should be mostly white and should not be sandles. Many ask about white "Crocs." These will usually be allowed, but not always. It is up to the meet referee.

In championship season, we change it up a little bit. Preliminary sessions will use the same white shirt, but khaki shorts, pants, or a skirt are needed. For finals sessions, it will be a navy blue shirt. Many times the meet announcement says that only khaki pants and skirts are allowed in finals (no shorts!). Check your meet announcement for details.

So, you're a certified Stroke & Turn official. What happens next?

It's up to you. If you wish, you can remain at the Stroke and Turn position and continue to have an impact on swimmers. You can also advance to other positions on deck. Do you like having all eyes on you? Starters get to be the centers of attention. If you're really into middle management, you can be a Chief Judge. Maybe you like air conditioning, chairs, and long hours in front computer screens? Administrative officiating might be right for you. Or, you can channel your inner five-year-old to see how loudly you can blow a whistle as a Referee.

  • Starter

    This is the most common position to pursue after obtaining a Stroke & Turn certification. The starter is responsible for [shockingly!] starting the heat in a fair, consistent manner. After the referee raises his or her arm, the pool belongs to the starter who delivers the "Take your marks" command with a calm, inviting tone. When the field is set, he or she presses the button that launches the heat.

  • Chief Judge

    The Chief Judge certification may also be pursued by certified Stroke & Turn officials. The "CJ" assists the Referee in vetting calls for accuracy as well as mentoring and caring for the well-being of other officials.

  • Administrative Official

    Admin Officials work before, during, and after meets to make sure swimmers are properly entered, heats are seeded, times are correct, and results are properly scored. Ever wondered how results get into Meet Mobile? What happens once your swimmer circles in for an event? What happens to all those timer sheets collected by runners? The Admin Officials are responsible for all of these things.

  • Referee

    This level comes in two flavors. As a Deck Referee, you are in charge of a pool. You control the pace of the meet and your job is to advocate for the swimmers by ensuring they are always given the benefit of the doubt. Experienced Deck Referees may wish to become Meet Referees. These are generally the most experienced local officials and take the responsibility for running entire meets. As a qualified Referee, you can do either job.

Introduction to OTS

The Officials Tracking System (OTS) is used to track meets, sessions, and certifications. Each time you work at a meet, the meet referee should give you credit for working at one or more sessions. He or she will also assign a role for each session. While you might work in multiple roles within a given session, OTS is only able to accept a single role. Coordinate with the meet referee to receive the session credit you desire.
